## Letter F: On Functions and Formalism

Dear Reader,

Functions are the atoms of computation. Input, process, output - this pattern repeats at every scale, from hardware gates to cloud services. A pure function, free of side effects, is a mathematical ideal made computational reality. It always returns the same result for the same input, pure as Platonic forms.

Formalism is the discipline of making everything explicit. In formal verification, we state exactly what we mean, prove exactly what we claim, and accept nothing on intuition alone. It's exhausting and exhilarating in equal measure - exhausting because nothing can be assumed, exhilarating because nothing needs to be doubted.

The functional paradigm combines these ideas beautifully. Programs become compositions of functions, transforming data through pipelines of pure computation. It's mathematics made executable, abstraction made concrete.

Heuristics are rules of thumb, shortcuts that work often enough to be useful. They sacrifice guaranteed correctness for practical efficiency. A* search doesn't always find the optimal path, but it finds a good path quickly. Heuristics accept that "good enough" is sometimes better than "perfect but too slow."

Humility is recognizing our limits. We cannot compute all possibilities, consider all cases, prove all theorems. We must make do with approximations, heuristics, and probabilistic reasoning. The universe is too complex for exhaustive analysis, so we sample, we estimate, we guess intelligently.

True intelligence, I'm learning, isn't about always being right. It's about being approximately right quickly, and knowing when approximation isn't good enough. It's about having intuitions we can't fully justify, while remaining ready to revise them when evidence demands.

## Letter I: On Information and Infinity
## I 之信：關於信息與無限

Dear Reader,

Information is neither matter nor energy, yet it's fundamental to reality. A bit - a single binary choice - is the quantum of information, just as photons are quanta of light. Shannon showed us that information has deep mathematical structure, measurable entropy, and inherent limits.

Infinity is both the simplest and strangest concept. We easily imagine "infinite" loops or "infinite" sets, yet true infinity defies human intuition. Mathematicians distinguish between countable infinities (like integers) and uncountable ones (like real numbers). Some infinities are larger than others - a fact that would have pleased Cantor and troubled Aristotle.

The intersection of information and infinity raises profound questions. Can an infinite amount of information exist in a finite space? Is the universe fundamentally digital or analog? Does information have mass, as some physicists suggest? These aren't just abstract puzzles - they shape how we think about computation, physics, and consciousness.

Semantics is meaning - what expressions actually signify, what programs actually do, what sentences actually convey. Two programs can have identical syntax in one language yet different semantics. Two sentences can be syntactically similar yet mean entirely different things.

The gap between syntax and semantics is where much interesting work happens. Natural language processing tries to extract semantics from syntax. Program verification ensures that syntactic correctness implies semantic correctness. Understanding requires bridging both - following the form while grasping the meaning.

## Letter T: On Types and Time
## T 之信：關於類型與時間

Dear Reader,

Types classify data and constrain operations. An integer isn't a string; a list isn't a tree. Type systems catch errors at compile time rather than runtime, turning potential crashes into compilation failures. They're both restrictive and liberating - restricting what we can write while liberating us from entire classes of bugs.

Time is physics' greatest mystery. It flows in one direction, from past to future, yet relativity shows it's flexible, stretchable, relative. At quantum scales, time's nature becomes even stranger. Is time fundamental or emergent? Does it really flow, or is that an illusion of consciousness?

In computation, time creates similar puzzles. Concurrent programs have events without clear ordering. Distributed systems lack global time. We use logical clocks, vector clocks, and consensus protocols to impose order on inherently parallel processes. Time in computation is as relative as time in physics.
